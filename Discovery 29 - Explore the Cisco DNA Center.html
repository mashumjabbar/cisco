  • Note
    The red callouts in the simulation signify the steps where you will simply observe. When the blue callouts appear, you are expected to follow the instructions, take action, and explore the Cisco DNA GUI.
    Complete the following steps:

  • Procedure1:
    Click the Cisco DNA Center icon.
    This action will automatically connect you to the Cisco DNA Center GUI home page, where you get the welcome message, overall health summary, network snapshot, network configuration, and operation and tools.
  • Procedure2:
    The screen scrolls down to the Tools area; click the Discovery box.
    This tool allows the Cisco DNA Center to automatically build a network topology.
    Cisco DNA Center offers three methods for discovering networking devices.
    • Cisco Discovery Protocol: Cisco DNA center uses Cisco Discovery protocol to discover network devices and interconnections between devices.
    • Range: You can provide an IP address range and the server will attempt to communicate with devices by using SSH, Telnet, HTTP, HTTPS, and NETCONF.
    • Link Layer Discovery Protocol: Cisco DNA center uses Link Layer Discovery protocol to discover network devices and interconnections between devices.
  • Procedure3:
    Click the DiscoveryAtAll Sites area to display the discovered devices.
    Regardless of the method that you use, you will need to provide the credentials to use when attempting communication from the server to get access to the information on the device. These credentials can be global (used for all devices) or specific (exception device credentials that are used during the scan).
    You can see that a couple of active scans are currently in progress. You can see that the IP addresses are listed in the table on the right-hand side, along with some detailed information that has been discovered about this device.
  • Procedure4:
    Click the Cisco icon in the top left corner to return to the Home screen; the screen will scroll down to Tools. Click the Inventory box, then click the three dots to display the Layout menu.
    This page displays all the network devices that the Cisco DNA Center controller is aware of and can work with.
    By default, the display shows the Status, Hardware, and Tagging layouts. When you use the Layout drop-down (three vertical dots) menu, you can define which type of information you want to be displayed. For example, media access control (MAC) address, IP address, operating system (OS) version, device platform, and serial number. You can also view the configuration of the device, the up time, and so on.
    You can also sort and filter devices by device name, IP address, reachability status, or the OS version.
  • Procedure5:
    Click the MAC address checkbox, and then click Apply.
    Notice that the MAC addresses have been added.
  • Procedure6:
    Click the Device Name column title to sort devices bottom-up.
    Before clicking the Device Name column:
    After clicking the Device Name column:
  • Procedure7:
    Click the Cisco icon in the top left corner; the screen will scroll down to Tools. Click the Topology box.
    This page gives you a visual depiction of what the network topology looks like, including all the nodes and edges that were gathered during the discovery process.
    There are multiple topological graphs. You can choose them by using the View Option gear and Layer menu.
  • Procedure8:
    In the Topology view, choose the View Option gear. In the Layout tab, the Cisco Recommended view is chosen by default. Click the Collapsed View to toggle the Expanded View and then click the Expanded View to toggle back.
    Observe how the topology is presented.
  • Procedure9:
    Click the Cisco icon in the top left corner to return to the Home screen, then choose the Policy option from the top menu.
    You can see Dashboard, Group-Based Access Control, IP Based Access Control, Application, Traffic Copy, and Virtual Network tabs.
  • Procedure10:
    Choose the IP Based Access Control tab. The IP Based Access Control Policies tab is already selected. Click Add Policy.
    You can see that the parameters here are similar to creating an access control list (ACL) on Cisco Internet Operating System (IOS). The only difference is that this policy will be pushed to all devices that need it, which helps you make fewer mistakes when implementing networkwide policies.
  • Procedure11:
    Now, choose the Assurance option from the top menu. The screen will scroll down and then scroll up again.
    This page gives you a visual depiction of the Overall Health, including Network Devices health, Wired Clients health, Wireless Clients health, and Top 10 Issues.
  • Procedure12:
    From the menu, choose Health > Network. The screen will scroll down and then scroll up again.
    The dashboard provides an at-a-glance view of the overall network health.
    Health Scores are given for the network and for clients. Cisco DNA Center pulls information from switches, routers, WLCs, and APs, correlating that information with the Cisco Identity Services Engine (ISE) which provides client information on both users and devices, making this dashboard view incredibly helpful in quickly identifying the health of the entire network environment.
  • Procedure13:
    Go to Issues > Global Issues.
    If problems have been detected on any of these elements, the Global Issues list enables you to quickly locate issues that may be impacting users or network performance.
  • Procedure14:
    You will notice that Cisco DNA Center can greatly simplify troubleshooting effort. For example, user Grace called saying that she was not able to connect to the network yesterday. Click the Search tool (the magnifying glass Icon) in the upper right corner, enter gra, and then press Enter, select user Grace.Smith and click the User 360. The screen will scroll to the Issues area.
    Since Cisco DNA Center is tied to the Cisco ISE, you will start by using the search tool to find Grace. After typing in a few letters, the search tool autocompletes the name to find Grace Smith. Now, take a look at what you can learn about Grace and her difficulties.
    With Client 360, you can see the complete Cisco DNA Center related information pertaining to Grace Smith. You can see that Grace has three devices registered and one of them is having trouble. Her iPad does not appear to be connected but her other devices are connected without issue.
  • Procedure15:
    Observe Grace's iPad Issues.
    You also see a list of issues that Grace has experienced. The issues listed are context-sensitive, meaning the issues are related to the device under examination, in this case, her iPad. It looks like she’s had couple of issues: Application issues and Onboarding issues.
  • Procedure16:
    Click Grace's iPad Onboarding. The screen will scroll down through the Detail information, and back up. The screen will then scroll through the timeline.
    You can see exactly where (her location) she was trying to connect. You are able to get the information about her location and about the specific devices that her iPad was interacting with when she had the problem throughout the onboarding process.
    You can also see that the Authentication Server was operational. You can automatically eliminate one possible source of the problem.
    Observe the Detail information related to Grace’s iPad and its experience on the network including her IP Address, MAC Address, what radio frequency band she is trying to connect with and so on.
    You have complete visibility. However, you need to know what was happening yesterday when she was trying to connect. One of the most difficult types of problems to resolve are problems that are reported now but happened in the past—which is exactly the purpose and power of Network Time Travel. Take a look and see how it works.
    Once at the top of the page again, you see her iPad timeline for the last 24 hours. You can change the granularity from 3 hours to 7 days; however, you are interested in the last 24 hours.
    Observing the timeline, you can see that Grace’s iPad was fine prior to the problem with a perfect health score and strong wireless connectivity.
  • Procedure17:
    When the cursor is where the health dropped to 1, click the timeline. The screen will scroll down to the Issues area.
    She was not connecting at this point.
  • Procedure18:
    Click the Wireless client failed to connect issue
    As you continue to explore the timeline, you also notice that the issues are, once again, context sensitive, meaning the issues listed reflect or are associated with the timeline under examination.
    Looking at the issues found by the analytic engine within Assurance you see that she failed to connect to Service Set Identifier (SSID) @CorpSSID because the client timed out.
    You have now completed this activity.
